    Stuart WardSignature Track· 8 days ago 
    It seems that the TA comments regarding the time to train the model for the course project are the opposite of "Practical" advice:

    "…it may take a long time. It may take a VERY long time. Or it may take an EXTREMELY long time to train your learning algorithm on your data. We are looking in the range between half an hour, to tens of hours, to potentially an entire day. That's just to train one single algorithm on the data you will be using for your assignment."

    I’ve just worked through the course project and scored 20/20 correct on the submission. Out of curiosity, I benchmarked the time to train a few different algorithms (randomForest, QDA, xgboost) utilizing a number of different system platforms (R on laptop; R on Amazon AWS, Python/scikit-learn on laptop; Python/scikit-learn on Azure ML; native Azure ML).

    Using R on Amazon AWS (which utilizes 1 core and 1 GB of RAM), the total randomForest training time was less than 2 minutes (which was the longest time out of all the benchmarks). And this produced a model with 99% accuracy and also produced the 20/20 correct score on the course project submission.

    Would it be more appropriate to advise that if it takes longer than a few minutes to train your model, you might want to review the data and/or your model/parameters? I would hate for someone to have their computer tied up for hours or overnight when that is not necessary --- and definitely not practical.

    Patricia Ellen TresselCommunity TA· 5 days ago 
    Hi, Stuart!  It's ok to offer advice that's about properly "using the tools".  Avoid code or equivalent that's about "doing the project".

    People have usually reported that the largest amount of time in training is consumed by allowing caret to do its default 25 rounds of bootstrap resampling or specifying too large a number of repeats or folds for CV, with whatever it chooses for a parameter tuning grid.  People usually start out wanting to let caret do as it pleases, but then start shedding repetitions as they realize it's going to take a long time to let caret do that.

    Now, with this dataset, we can get away with not letting caret do much model tuning, or having it pick based on only a few CV folds, but more normal datasets may need that model tuning and may need to do more folds of CV to get better accuracies for comparing sets of model parameters.  Our dataset has a peculiarity that lets us get insanely high accuracy (which we should not expect to get on other datasets) using only the real predictors (no bookkeeping variables), even without much model tuning and without needed to train on most of the data.  (After the project due date, we're going to revisit that question and ask folks to have a go at answering it.  But not yet -- wait til folks get their work done.)

    Another issue may be keeping lots of big objects in R's memory, that aren't immediately needed for the particular classifier being trained.  Those can be written out and rm()'d, so the training gets as much of the available memory as it can.

    Also, some folks just don't have very high-powered gear to run on, and R may be competing for resources with other processes that are hard to get rid of or turn off while R is running.  One advantage to an AWS Linux system is that essentially nothing else significant will be running -- they don't start up a bunch of extraneous Linux daemons by default, if you choose a basic AMI.  (On my system, if my cloud backup starts running, it will provoke my AV into doing its scan of everything being read.  And if I've got Firefox up, that's one core and about 1.5GB of memory tied up right there (yes, I have a lot of windows and tabs open...).  Then MS might decide to push some important security fix out of schedule, and there goes another big wodge of resources.  Or the storage manager might decide to defrag the disk just right now.  Or the local backup might go into a tizzy because its backup disk isn't attached.  Or the AV might say, now is a good time to scan the disk.  Or...)

    (Re. predicting with the bookkeeping variables...or rather, with num_window in particular:  An amusing thing to try is to use a plain vanilla non-CART decision tree like C5.0 and use only num_window as a predictor.  Then look at the tree it generates.)

    Stuart WardSignature Track· 3 days ago 
    Validation advice: K-fold cross validation is especially beneficial when your data set is limited in size, but as the number of samples increases, then the size of K can be significantly decreased. Or in the case of large, dense, robust data sets, a simple random stratified hold-out validation set (70/30 split of training data) may be all that is necessary to train and validate a highly accurate model.

    Caret advice: similar to the advice for validation, if your data set is small in size and scope, the defaults for caret can work quite well. As the data set increases in size and complexity then the caret package defaults can lead to unnecessarily long training times. To prevent this, you must finely control the settings, such as for cross validation and mtry (as an example for training a random forest model in caret). A simpler option might be to bypass the caret package and utilize the machine learning algorithm directly (such as the "randomForest" package).

    Model advice: it has been suggested when approaching a new machine learning challenge, to first pick an algorithm that trains very quickly to set a benchmark to work from. For example, applied to a large, dense data set (say, one with tens of thousands of rows and dozens of columns), a QDA model can be trained in less than one second (using a machine with 1 core and 1 GB of RAM) with very high predictive accuracy. Once a benchmark has been set you can venture on to other models with traditionally higher accuracy rates (such as Random Forests), while knowing there will be trade-offs in training time (and as I referenced above, these time trade-offs should be in minutes, not hours or longer).

    Even though this graphic (http://scikit-learn.org/stable/auto_examples/classification/plot_classifier_comparison.html) was created on a synthetic data set, I find it helpful to visualize the decision boundaries and compare accuracy rates for a number of algorithms.
